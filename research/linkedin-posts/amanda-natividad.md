## Post 1

Link: https://www.linkedin.com/posts/amandanat_every-marketer-wants-to-know-whats-my-audience-activity-7441868964948967424-GMZW
Date: 3 months ago
Topic: Difference between Google searches and AI prompts for audience research

### Summary
- Amanda shares SparkToro data showing what a specific audience is likely typing into AI tools.
- She compares AI prompt behavior with Google search behavior for forward-thinking B2B content marketing professionals.
- The audience’s Google searches appear more tactical and specific, while their AI prompts are broader and more strategic.
- Her key point is that search behavior and prompt behavior are different.
- Traditional SEO content may need to remain specific, tactical, and how-to oriented.
- Content optimized for AI answers may need to be more strategic, framework-driven, and opinionated.

### Tactical Takeaway
- B2B SaaS teams should not treat AI prompts exactly like Google keywords.
- They should research both traditional search queries and AI prompt topics, then create different content formats for each behavior.
- SEO content can target specific tactical queries, while AI-visible content should answer broader strategic questions with frameworks, opinions, and decision guidance.

### B2B SaaS Relevance
- This is highly relevant for B2B SaaS because buyers often use Google and AI tools for different jobs.
- A SaaS buyer may search Google for specific terms like pricing, integrations, alternatives, or implementation steps.
- The same buyer may use ChatGPT or Claude to ask broader questions like which strategy to choose, what tools to compare, or how to solve a business problem.
- SaaS teams should build content around both behaviors rather than relying only on keyword research.

### AI SEO / Content Relevance
- This supports the idea that AI-powered SEO content production should be based on audience prompt research, not only keyword research.
- It shows that AI content should be more strategic, framework-based, and useful for decision-making.
- It also supports the need to map buyer questions across both search engines and AI assistants.

### Risk or Limitation
- Audience prompt data should not be treated as a perfect picture of every buyer’s behavior.
- Prompt behavior can vary by audience, industry, tool, and intent.
- Teams should validate prompt research with customer interviews, sales calls, support tickets, CRM notes, community discussions, and search data.

### Use in Future Playbook
- This source can support a playbook section on AI prompt research versus keyword research.
- It can also support workflows for building AI-visible content around strategic prompts, buyer questions, frameworks, category education, and decision-stage topics.

## Post 2

Link: https://www.linkedin.com/posts/amandanat_even-when-ai-is-wrong-about-you-youre-still-activity-7475197236998533120-Ll94
Date: 4 days ago
Topic: AI visibility, positioning signals, and public brand record

### Summary
- Amanda explains that even when AI tools describe a brand incorrectly, the mistake can still be useful.
- She gives the example of SparkToro being compared with companies like Ahrefs, Semrush, Similarweb, and Brandwatch, even though SparkToro is not the same type of product.
- Her point is that AI systems absorb public context from across the internet.
- Positioning is not only what a brand says on its homepage. It is also shaped by third-party articles, reviews, comparisons, audience perception, and market context.
- Instead of dismissing AI mistakes as hallucinations, brands should ask why the association happened.
- The mistake may reveal unclear category positioning, confusing comparison signals, or third-party content that frames the brand incorrectly.

### Tactical Takeaway
- B2B SaaS teams should run AI visibility checks not only to see whether they appear, but also to see how AI systems describe and compare them.
- If AI tools associate the brand with the wrong competitors or category, the team should investigate whether the public record is creating confusion.
- This can guide positioning, content updates, comparison pages, third-party mentions, and category clarification.

### B2B SaaS Relevance
- This is highly relevant for B2B SaaS because positioning and competitive comparison strongly influence buying decisions.
- SaaS buyers often ask AI tools which vendors to compare, which category a product belongs to, and which alternative is best.
- If AI systems misunderstand the brand’s category or competitors, the brand may lose visibility or appear in the wrong buying journey.
- SaaS companies need to make their category, use cases, audience, differentiators, and alternatives clear across owned and third-party sources.

### AI SEO / Content Relevance
- This supports the idea that AI SEO is also about representation accuracy.
- AI-powered SEO content production should help AI systems understand what the product is, who it is for, what category it belongs to, what it replaces, and how it differs from competitors.
- It also connects AI visibility with positioning, public mentions, comparison content, and third-party context.

### Risk or Limitation
- Not every AI mistake reflects a real positioning problem. Some errors may come from model limitations or temporary hallucinations.
- Teams should not rewrite strategy based on one AI output.
- AI visibility checks should be repeated across multiple prompts, tools, markets, and comparison contexts before making major positioning decisions.

### Use in Future Playbook
- This source can support a playbook section on AI representation accuracy.
- It can also support workflows for checking whether AI tools understand the brand’s category, audience, competitors, differentiators, and use cases.
- The playbook can include a process for fixing unclear positioning through homepage copy, comparison pages, review site updates, third-party content, PR, and expert mentions.

## Patterns Observed
- Amanda’s content focuses on how audience behavior is changing across search, AI tools, and zero-click environments.
- She emphasizes that Google searches and AI prompts are not the same. Search queries are often tactical, while AI prompts can be broader and more strategic.
- Her work connects AI visibility with audience research, positioning, content strategy, and public brand perception.
- She treats AI mistakes as diagnostic signals that can reveal unclear positioning or confusing third-party context.
- Amanda’s approach suggests that brands need to understand how buyers search, prompt, compare, and form opinions before clicking.
- Her content supports a more strategic approach to AI SEO where content is built around buyer questions, prompt behavior, category clarity, and representation accuracy.
- A recurring theme is that marketers should not outsource judgment to AI. They should use AI data and outputs as inputs for better strategy.

## Notes for Future Playbook
- Research both traditional search queries and AI prompts because buyers use them differently.
- Use SEO content for specific, tactical, high-intent queries.
- Use AI-visible content for strategic, framework-driven, opinionated, and decision-support topics.
- Build a prompt research workflow around audience segments, buying stages, use cases, competitor comparisons, category questions, and strategic problems.
- Track how AI tools describe the brand, not only whether they mention it.
- Audit whether AI systems understand the brand’s category, use cases, competitors, alternatives, and differentiators correctly.
- Treat AI mistakes as diagnostic clues, then validate them across multiple tools and prompts before acting.
- Improve representation accuracy through clearer owned content, comparison pages, third-party mentions, review profiles, thought leadership, and category education.
- Combine audience research, prompt research, and positioning work before creating large volumes of AI-assisted SEO content.

## Post 1

Link: https://www.linkedin.com/posts/thinkingslow_does-ai-content-work-is-entirely-the-wrong-activity-7470769358055825408-X1Wn
Date: 2 weeks ago
Topic: AI content quality, editorial standards, and risk of scaling too fast

### Summary
- Ryan argues that “Does AI content work?” is the wrong question.
- A better question is whether AI-generated content is materially different from normal high-quality content.
- He explains that many teams use AI in ways that create obvious quality gaps: publishing too fast, relying only on the AI model’s internal knowledge, skipping external sources, avoiding visuals, ignoring links, and leaving obvious AI writing patterns.
- These patterns can make content worse than traditional editorial content and easier to identify as low-quality or AI-generated.
- Ryan says Ahrefs uses generative AI, but only when the AI process mirrors the human editorial process step by step.
- His main argument is that AI should be used to create better, more useful, and more interesting content, not cheaper or weaker content at scale.

### Tactical Takeaway
- B2B SaaS teams should not use AI only to increase publishing speed.
- They should use AI inside a strong editorial workflow that includes research, sourcing, internal links, external links, visual assets, examples, brand voice, expert input, and editorial quality control.

### B2B SaaS Relevance
- This is highly relevant for B2B SaaS because SaaS content often competes in crowded categories where many companies publish similar keyword-focused articles.
- AI can help SaaS teams improve research, update old content, analyze product data, create comparisons, and improve content depth.
- But if AI is used only to mass-produce generic articles, it can weaken trust, search performance, and brand differentiation.

### AI SEO / Content Relevance
- This supports the idea that AI-powered SEO content production should be treated as an editorial system, not a shortcut.
- AI content should still meet normal SEO and content quality standards: originality, sourcing, usefulness, internal linking, external references, visuals, examples, and clear brand voice.
- The post also shows that AI can improve content quality when used to remove research, data, design, and updating constraints.

### Risk or Limitation
- The post reflects Ryan’s opinion and Ahrefs’ editorial approach, so it should not be treated as a universal rule for every website.
- AI content risk may depend on domain authority, brand demand, content quality, publishing velocity, topic sensitivity, and Google’s ability to detect low-quality patterns.
- Early-stage SaaS companies should be careful about scaling AI content before they have authority, trust, and a clear editorial process.

### Use in Future Playbook
- This source can support a playbook section on AI content quality control.
- It can also support an AI content production workflow where AI is used for research, outlining, updating, examples, and structure, while human editors maintain strategy, judgment, brand voice, and final quality.

## Post 2

Link: https://www.linkedin.com/posts/thinkingslow_in-the-last-3-months-at-ahrefs-we-analyzed-activity-7424382429542486016--OIQ
Date: 4 months ago
Topic: AI search optimization insights from Ahrefs research

### Summary
- Ryan shares findings from Ahrefs research based on more than 1 billion data points across 11 studies.
- One key finding is that YouTube mentions were strongly correlated with AI visibility, stronger than several traditional SEO factors.
- He explains that AI Mode and AI Overviews often reach similar conclusions, but they cite very different sources.
- The post suggests that content length has weak correlation with AI citations, meaning longer content is not always necessary for AI visibility.
- Freshness appears important because many cited pages were recently updated.
- Ryan also notes that “best X” list posts are commonly cited in ChatGPT responses.
- Traditional SEO authority metrics like Domain Rating may not fully explain AI visibility.

### Tactical Takeaway
- B2B SaaS teams should not assume that traditional SEO signals alone will drive AI visibility.
-They should test visibility across AI search surfaces, monitor citations, refresh important pages regularly, and consider non-blog sources such as YouTube, comparison pages, and list-style content.

### B2B SaaS Relevance
- This is highly relevant for B2B SaaS because SaaS buyers often use AI tools to compare products, find “best tools,” evaluate alternatives, and shortlist vendors.
- The finding around “best X” content is useful because SaaS categories often depend heavily on list posts, comparison pages, and vendor roundups.
- The YouTube insight is also important because SaaS teams may need to think beyond blog SEO and build visibility through videos, demos, explainers, podcasts, and third-party mentions.

### AI SEO / Content Relevance
- This post supports the idea that AI SEO is broader than keyword-focused blogging.
- AI-powered SEO content production should include freshness, citation tracking, comparison content, multimedia visibility, and source diversity.
- It also reinforces that content structure and source format may matter differently in AI search than in traditional Google rankings.

### Risk or Limitation
- The findings are based on Ahrefs research and may vary by industry, query type, platform, and time.
- Correlation does not always prove causation, so teams should not assume that creating YouTube content automatically causes AI visibility.
- AI search systems change quickly, so these patterns should be monitored over time rather than treated as permanent rules.

### Use in Future Playbook
- This source can support a playbook section on AI search optimization signals.
- It can also support workflows for tracking AI citations, refreshing important content, creating comparison/list pages, testing YouTube visibility, and measuring AI search presence beyond traditional SEO metrics.

## Patterns Observed
- Ryan’s content focuses on using AI to improve content quality, not simply to publish faster.
- He separates good AI-assisted content from low-quality AI-scaled content.
- His view is that AI should fit into a strong editorial process with research, sourcing, examples, visuals, links, brand voice, and human judgment.
- He also emphasizes that AI search visibility may depend on different signals than traditional SEO, including YouTube visibility, freshness, citations, source diversity, and list-style content.
- Traditional SEO metrics like Domain Rating, backlinks, and content length may not fully explain AI visibility.
- For B2B SaaS, the strongest opportunity is not mass-producing generic content, but building better content systems, stronger comparison assets, fresher pages, and more trusted source coverage.

## Notes for Future Playbook
- Do not treat AI content as a shortcut. Treat it as a production system inside a strong editorial workflow.
- Use AI for research, briefs, outlining, content updates, examples, data analysis, and structure, but keep human judgment in strategy, positioning, quality control, and final editing.
- Avoid scaling AI content too quickly on weak domains with low authority, no brand demand, and no editorial standards.
- Create content that is at least as good as human-produced content, with clear sourcing, internal links, external references, visuals, examples, and useful insights.
- Track AI visibility separately from traditional SEO rankings because AI systems may cite different sources from normal search results.
- Refresh important SaaS pages regularly, especially comparison, alternatives, “best tools,” category, and decision-stage content.
- Test non-blog visibility channels such as YouTube, podcasts, demos, webinars, and third-party mentions because AI systems may use multiple source types.
- Build AI content workflows that improve quality and usefulness, not just speed and volume.
